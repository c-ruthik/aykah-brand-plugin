Executive Summary
Why we're repositioning, what changes, and what stays the same.
The Opportunity

Aykah is launching a new premium product line at $1,600–$3,000+ CAD, moving upmarket from our current $500–$2,200 range. This requires a complete repositioning of our brand voice, visual language, and marketing strategy to match the expectations of affluent Canadian customers. Our current AOV of $945 and strong 4.8/5 rating across 443 reviews prove we already deliver premium quality — our language just needs to match.

Pricing Psychology — Why Round Numbers

Research-Backed: Zhang & Wadhwa (2015), Journal of Consumer Research

Rounded prices ($1,600) encourage reliance on feelings and are preferred for emotion-driven purchases like furniture. Non-rounded prices ($1,599.99) trigger rational/cognitive evaluation — the "discount store" signal. Louis Vuitton, Rolex, and Hermes all use round-number pricing exclusively. Aykah should move all pricing to round numbers immediately.

"Quiet Luxury" — Our Tailwind

Quiet luxury is THE defining interior design trend of 2025-2026 (confirmed by Decorilla, Homes & Gardens, Navilize, Castle Furniture). Core characteristics: matte finishes, natural materials (linen, boucle, wood), warm neutrals, no logos, craftsmanship focus. Aykah's color palette (navy, ivory, gold) and aesthetic already align perfectly. This is a massive market tailwind for our repositioning.

6 Things That Hurt Affluent Customer Ego

1. Assumed Financial Limitation
Never say "affordable," "budget-friendly," or "for every budget." Affluent buyers don't want to be reminded of budgets.
2. Herd Mentality Claims
"Thousands sold!" or "trending" signals mass-market. Affluent buyers want distinction, not popularity.
3. Over-Explanation
"Did you know...?" or "Simply put..." is condescending. Respect their intelligence.
4. Desperate Enthusiasm
ALL CAPS, exclamation marks, emojis signal desperation. Premium brands are calm and confident.
5. The Aspirational Trap
"World-class luxury" without substance. Actually premium brands SHOW quality through specs, not claim it with vague superlatives.
6. Transactional Framing
"Money-back guarantee" or hard-sell CTAs. Premium is relational, not transactional.

Section 10
Website Redesign Direction
What premium furniture websites look like in 2026 — and how Aykah should follow.

Key Design Principles

Whitespace
30-50% more whitespace than current. 120-200px between sections. Text max 600-700px wide. This is the #1 differentiator between premium and mass-market.
Imagery
Full-bleed hero images. 8-12 photos per product. Mix: styled room (3-4), silo (2-3), detail/material close-ups (2-3), scale reference (1-2).
Typography
Large serif headings (50-80px hero), dramatic size contrast with 16-18px body text. Serif headings signal premium — exactly what our font pairing lab is solving.
Navigation
4-6 links max. Mega-menu with category images on hover. Sticky nav that appears on scroll. Prominent search.

Homepage Hero — Recommended Approach

Full-screen lifestyle image, minimal text overlay. Collection name in elegant serif type. One CTA button ("Explore the Collection"). No cluttered messaging. Reference: RH, Poliform, Minotti.

Product Pages — Premium Standard

Add to Cart stays (transparency builds trust at $1,600-$3,000), but add a secondary CTA: "Book a Design Consultation". This is the RH hybrid model — self-serve + service. Specs in expandable accordions below the fold with narrative material descriptions.

Critical Fix: Assembly Experience

Risk: Assembly is the #1 customer complaint (443 reviews)

Words like "nightmare," "complex," and "hard" appear repeatedly in Aykah reviews. Premium brands either ship pre-assembled or provide white-glove service. For the premium line, Aykah must offer optional white-glove delivery and assembly, or dramatically improve instructions and part labelling. This is non-negotiable for premium repositioning.

3-Email Premium Launch Sequence

Email 1 — The Tease (5-7 days before)
"Something new is coming." One detail close-up. 2-3 sentences. No product names or prices. "Be the first to know" CTA.
Email 2 — The Reveal (Launch day)
"Introducing the [Collection Name]." Full hero image. Collection story. 3-4 product images. "Discover the Collection" CTA.
Email 3 — The Deep Dive (3-5 days post)
"The details behind [Collection Name]." Material stories, design process. Individual product spotlights. Converts browsers to buyers.

Cadence: 6-8 emails per month maximum

Never more than 2 emails per week. Each email should feel like an event, not noise. Premium brands email less frequently than mass-market. Single-column layout, 500-600px wide, ivory background (#FAF8F4).
