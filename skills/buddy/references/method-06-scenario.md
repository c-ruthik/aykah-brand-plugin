# Method 6 — Scenario Wind-Tunnel

**Source:** Pierre Wack / Royal Dutch Shell (1970s). Wack's scenarios famously let Shell anticipate the 1973 oil crisis. Mechanism: changes decision-makers' "microcosm" (mental model), not predicting outcomes.

---

## What it does

Tests strategic decisions against multiple plausible futures. The point isn't predicting the future — it's stress-testing decisions across several futures so you don't bet the company on a single forecast.

## When to use

Multi-year strategic decisions where the payoff depends on assumptions about how the world will look in 1–5 years:

- "Should we open Calgary in 2027?"
- "Should we sunset the value tier and go all-premium?"
- "Should we hire a full-time CMO?"
- "Should we move warehousing in-house?"
- Major brand-direction shifts, market entries, big infrastructure investments

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Frame the strategic question

**Verb + noun + time horizon.**

- Bad: "What should we do about Aykah?"
- Good: "Should Aykah open a Calgary showroom in late 2026?"

If the question isn't clearly framed, route back to Method 1.

### Step 2 — Identify the two most uncertain critical drivers

Two forces that are both:

- **High impact** — would change the answer if they shifted
- **High uncertainty** — we don't know which way they'll go

These become the two axes of a 2x2 matrix. Each quadrant becomes a different future.

Examples for Aykah:

- *Furniture e-commerce maturity in Canada* (consolidation vs. fragmentation)
- *Consumer willingness to pay premium* (rising vs. compressing)
- *Showroom-led conversion vs. digital-only* (traffic shifts)
- *Materials-cost inflation* (stable vs. volatile)
- *AI-generated visual ad costs* (cheap vs. saturated and ignored)

Pick two for any single Wind-Tunnel session.

### Step 3 — Build 4 internally consistent scenarios

For each quadrant, write a 4–6 sentence story. Each must be:

- **Internally consistent** — every element holds together
- **Plausible** — not a wild card
- **Distinct** — qualitatively different from the others
- **Named** — memorable. "Open Field" sticks where "Scenario A" doesn't.

### Step 4 — Test the proposed strategy against each scenario

For each scenario score:

- Does it succeed, survive, struggle, or fail?
- What's the cost of being wrong here?
- What signals would tell us this scenario is unfolding?

A **robust** strategy works in 3 of 4 scenarios. A **brittle** one only works in 1–2.

### Step 5 — Look for robust moves, convertible strategies, and hedges

- **Robust moves** — work in every scenario (e.g., a better online configurator works regardless of the Calgary showroom decision)
- **Convertible strategies** — start in a way that can be reshaped as scenarios become clearer (lease 4,000 sqft with expansion clause vs. 8,000 sqft outright)
- **Hedges** — explicitly preparing for worst-case so its damage is contained (3-year lease vs. 7-year)

### Step 6 — Identify leading indicators

For each scenario, pick 2–3 signals you'd see early if that future were unfolding. These become a monitoring dashboard. Wack called this *"memory of the future."*

## Output format

```
Strategic question: <verb + noun + time horizon>

Two uncertain critical drivers:
  Axis 1: ...
  Axis 2: ...

Four scenarios:
  Scenario 1 — "<name>" (Axis 1 high + Axis 2 high)
    Story: <4–6 sentences>
    Strategy outcome: succeed / survive / struggle / fail
    Leading indicators: ...
  Scenario 2 — ...
  Scenario 3 — ...
  Scenario 4 — ...

Robustness summary: <strategy works in N of 4>

Robust moves: <list>
Convertible moves: <list>
Hedges: <list>

Verdict:
  - <accept / reject / modify / defer> the proposed strategy
  - Confidence: low / medium / high
  - Reshape recommendation: <one paragraph if "modify">
  - Monitoring dashboard: <2–3 leading indicators per scenario>
```

This output is long. Ask the user before saving — do not assume a path.

## Common failure modes

- Picking drivers that aren't actually uncertain (just dressed-up sensitivity analysis)
- Scenarios that are just optimistic / pessimistic versions of the same future
- Stories that aren't internally consistent
- Skipping leading indicators (without signposts, the wind-tunnel produces unactionable insight)
- Treating the verdict as binary yes/no (most decisions get reshaped, not killed)
