# Method 10 — StoryBrand SB7 (Aykah application)

Source: Donald Miller, *Building a StoryBrand* (2017). Invoked for `event-title`, `event-tagline`, and narrative `campaign` outputs. Casts every marketing message as a story where **the customer is the hero** and Aykah is the **guide**.

## The 7-part structure (Aykah-specific)

### 1. A character (the customer)

The Premium Practical ICP. Wants a home that feels like theirs. Mid-30s to mid-40s. New homeowner or upgrader.

### 2. Has a problem

- **External problem (visible):** Their current furniture is wrong — too small, sagging, outdated, mismatched.
- **Internal problem (felt):** They feel afraid to commit to expensive pieces. They don't trust photos. They've been burned before.
- **Philosophical problem:** A home should feel curated, not catalogued.

Most marketing only addresses External. The emotional weight lives in Internal + Philosophical.

### 3. Meets a guide (Aykah)

Aykah shows up with two things:

- **Empathy:** "We've spent 8 years figuring out what actually lasts. We know what it's like to spend $1,200 on something that turns out to be MDF."
- **Authority:** "Showrooms in Burnaby and Mississauga. Direct supply relationships with our factory in Vietnam since 2018. 12,000+ pieces shipped."

Both halves are required. Authority alone reads cold. Empathy alone reads like every other DTC brand.

### 4. Who gives them a plan

- **Step 1:** Visit a showroom (Burnaby or Mississauga)
- **Step 2:** Sit on it. Take a fabric swatch home if needed.
- **Step 3:** Order with confidence. Ships in 5 days from BC, longer from ON.

The plan reduces Anxiety (the dominant Force for Aykah buyers — see `01-jtbd.md`).

### 5. And calls them to action

Direct CTA, no urgency tricks:

- "Book a showroom visit"
- "Request a fabric swatch"
- "See it in real homes"
- "Reserve your spot at the [event]"

NO "Buy now," NO "Limited time," NO "Don't miss out."

### 6. That helps them avoid failure

What happens if they don't act:

- Living another year with furniture that doesn't feel like home
- Ordering blind from a competitor and getting MDF behind a veneer
- Spending more on a piece they can't sit on first
- Settling for a sectional that won't fit the new space

The failure is concrete. It's their story, not Aykah's.

### 7. And ends in success

The better life on the other side:

- A space that's quietly, unmistakably theirs
- Pieces that wear in over a decade, not out
- The home they pictured when they bought the house
- A dining table that earns compliments from guests

## The brand-as-hero failure (avoid this)

The most common StoryBrand failure: Aykah accidentally becomes the hero.

**Brand-as-hero (WRONG):**
> "We believe every home deserves furniture that lasts. That's why we partnered directly with our factory in Vietnam to bring you direct-from-factory pricing on solid hardwood. Our showrooms in Burnaby and Mississauga are the result of years of work…"

The "we" anchor makes Aykah the hero. Customer is reduced to passive recipient.

**Customer-as-hero (RIGHT):**
> "You'll know the chair the moment you sit in it. Solid oak frame, bouclé that softens. The kind of dining chair people compliment without realizing you ordered it online. Sit on it at our Burnaby showroom — you'll know."

The "you" anchor makes the customer the hero. Aykah is the guide showing them the way.

## When to use StoryBrand

| Use case | StoryBrand fit |
|---|---|
| Homepage hero section | ✅ Strong fit |
| About Us page | ✅ Strong fit |
| Founder story video | ✅ Strong fit |
| Email welcome sequence | ✅ Strong fit |
| Event title / event tagline | ✅ Strong fit (anchors customer-as-hero) |
| Long-form campaign concept | ✅ Strong fit |
| Stage-5 product ads | ❌ Skip — too narrative for direct response |
| Quick social posts | ❌ Skip — compresses too far |
| Subject lines | ⚠️ Use the structure as a check, not as the line itself |

## Generating event titles using StoryBrand

The event title should imply the customer's journey, not Aykah's. Instead of "Aykah's Spring Sale", try:

- "Reset Your Space" (customer's plan)
- "The Year-Three Refresh" (customer's situation)
- "Sit On It Weekend" (customer's plan, anxiety-reducing)
- "Quietly Yours" (customer's success state)

Each centers the buyer, not the brand.

## Generating event taglines using StoryBrand

The tagline can hold one or more SB7 elements:

- Problem + Plan: "Pick the chair. Sit on it. Bring it home." (External + Plan)
- Plan + Success: "Sit on it. See yourself there. Order with confidence." (Plan + Success)
- Failure-avoidance + Success: "Stop guessing. Start hosting." (Failure + Success)

## Common StoryBrand failures

- Brand sneaks back as hero — "we believe..." is the giveaway
- Skipping internal/philosophical problems — emotional weight lives there
- Writing "guide" voice that's preachy ("we've helped thousands of families…") instead of empathetic ("you've probably been here before…")
- Soft "failure to avoid" — "we want you to be happy" doesn't tell the customer what they lose by not acting
- Using SB7 in Stage-5 contexts where direct response is the right tool
