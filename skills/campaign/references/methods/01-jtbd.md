# Method 1 — Jobs to be Done (Aykah application)

Source: Christensen / Ulwick / Moesta. Loaded as grounding for every `/aykah:campaign` invocation.

## The three jobs (always layered)

Every Aykah purchase has three simultaneous jobs:

- **Functional** — the practical task. Furnish a dining room. Replace the broken sectional. Make a 1,400 sq ft living room feel finished.
- **Emotional** — how they want to feel. Competent. In control. Not ripped off. Like an adult who made a grown-up choice.
- **Social** — how they want to be seen. Taste. Status. Belonging. "Looks like they hired a designer without hiring one."

When generating any campaign output, name which of the three jobs the output is targeting. If you can't name it, the output is too generic.

## Aykah segments — pre-mapped jobs

### Segment A — First-time furnishing (new home, no prior big purchase)

- Functional: furnish the new dining room / living room / bedroom from scratch within 3 months
- Emotional: not get scammed; not waste $5K on something that looks wrong in person
- Social: have a home that looks "considered" not "catalogued"

### Segment B — Upgrade buyer (graduating from IKEA / Wayfair)

- Functional: replace specific pieces that are showing wear — usually after 5–8 years
- Emotional: prove to themselves they've moved up; "I'm not a college student anymore"
- Social: signal taste without signaling "I have $20K in my chequing account"

### Segment C — Move-in (recently moved, partial replacement)

- Functional: fill specific gaps — sectional doesn't fit, dining table too small for new room
- Emotional: get the new place to feel like home in time for hosting
- Social: pieces that show off the new home in photos / when guests come

### Segment D — Renovator (existing homeowner, rebuilding a room)

- Functional: pieces that match the design intent of the renovation
- Emotional: justify the renovation budget by getting the furniture right
- Social: show off the project; furniture is what guests actually see

Most Aykah ICP buyers are in B or C. A is the fastest-growing. D is the highest-LTV.

## Forces of Progress (Bob Moesta)

Every purchase is a fight between four forces. Most marketing only works on Pull. The biggest unlock is reducing Anxiety.

| Force | Aykah Premium Practical buyer |
|---|---|
| **Push** of current situation | Hand-me-down dining table doesn't fit the new house; sectional is sagging; college furniture looks wrong now that we own the place |
| **Pull** of new solution | Direct-from-factory means oak instead of MDF for the same price; showroom = sit before commit; designer-feel without designer pricing |
| **Anxiety** about the new | "What if it shows up looking nothing like the photos?"; "What if the bouclé gets dirty in 6 months?"; "What if returns are a nightmare?" |
| **Habit** of present | "We've eaten on the old table for years — we could keep doing it"; "Wayfair is fine for now" |

To make the sale: Push + Pull must outweigh Anxiety + Habit.

For Aykah specifically, **Anxiety is the dominant blocker** — not Pull. The buyer wants the chair. They're scared the chair won't be right. Marketing time spent on more "pull" features (oak frame, bouclé fabric, designer feel) yields less than marketing time spent on anxiety reduction (showroom visits, real customer photos, generous returns, materials transparency).

## How to use this in campaign generation

When generating any candidate (tagline, hook, subject line, campaign concept):

1. Pick the **dominant job** the output is targeting (functional / emotional / social).
2. Pick the **dominant Force** the output is moving (Push / Pull / Anxiety / Habit).
3. If the output addresses Pull only and the segment's blocker is Anxiety, drop the candidate.
4. Anxiety-reducing outputs almost always outperform pull-amplifying outputs for Aykah.

## Examples

### Hook addressing Pull (weak — only works on Stage 4–5 audiences)
> "Solid oak dining chair. Bouclé seat. $499."

### Hook addressing Anxiety (strong for cold + retargeting)
> "Sit on it before you commit. Burnaby + Mississauga showrooms."

### Tagline addressing emotional job (strong)
> "The grown-up dining table you've been waiting for."

### Tagline addressing social job (strong for Pinterest / IG saves)
> "What people compliment without realizing you ordered it online."

### Tagline addressing functional job only (weak — too generic)
> "Quality dining furniture, direct from the factory."

## Common failures to avoid

- Writing copy that targets all three jobs at once — diluted.
- Writing copy that targets Pull when the segment's blocker is Anxiety — wastes the impression.
- Writing copy without naming which job it serves — generic by default.
- Treating jobs as static — the same person has different jobs at different life stages. Match the campaign to the segment's current job.
