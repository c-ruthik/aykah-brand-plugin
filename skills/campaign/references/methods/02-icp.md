# Method 2 — ICP (Aykah application)

Source: standard ICP discipline + April Dunford's "best-fit customer" frame. Loaded as grounding for every `/aykah:campaign` invocation.

## The Aykah ICP — "Premium Practical"

Lives in BC or Ontario. Owns or just bought a 1,400–2,800 sq ft home. Household income $120K+. 32–48 years old. Has at least one child or is planning one. Follows interior accounts on Instagram, saves to Pinterest. Has shopped Wayfair and IKEA before but is graduating out — wants pieces that "last 10 years not 3." Reads design content (Architectural Digest, Apartment Therapy, NYT Real Estate) but does NOT use designers. Comparison shops between Cozey, Article, West Elm. Will drive 30 minutes to sit on a sofa before buying. Hates being upsold. Will pay 20% more for the right piece, will not pay 100% more.

This narrative is the source of truth. If a campaign candidate doesn't speak to this person, drop it.

## Behavioral signals — what tells you they're ready

- Saved 5+ furniture pins in the last 30 days
- Visited a Cozey or Article showroom
- Searched "[product type] vs [product type]" on Google
- Reads the materials section before reading the price
- Bookmarks a product, doesn't buy, comes back 3+ times
- Asks about delivery dates before checkout
- Requests a fabric swatch
- Books a showroom visit

When generating outputs, prefer hooks/CTAs that speak to actions this person is already doing — "request a swatch", "book a showroom visit", "see it in real homes" — over abstract brand-building.

## NOT-our-ICP segments

Drop candidates that target these audiences. They are explicitly out of scope:

- **Full-budget interior design clients ($50K+)** — they hire designers, not us
- **Pure-DTC younger buyers under 30** — wrong price band, wrong taste, wrong attention budget
- **IKEA-loyal buyers under $80K HHI** — won't pay our price, would feel buyer's remorse
- **Contractor-flippers buying for resale** — race to bottom, no taste signal, low LTV
- **Designer-trade buyers** — different program, different pricing, different positioning
- **Single-renters in studio apartments** — wrong life-stage, wrong budget, wrong space

If a candidate output reads like it could ONLY appeal to one of these segments, drop it. If a candidate output reads like it could appeal to ANYONE (no specificity), drop it for being generic.

## ICP fit test for any candidate

Score every candidate against the Premium Practical narrative:

1. Does this sound like something a 35-year-old BC homeowner with a Pinterest board would screenshot? → If no, fail.
2. Does this sound like something they'd send to their partner / spouse? → If no, fail.
3. Does this sound like Cozey/Article energy? → If yes, fail (drift).
4. Does this sound like Restoration Hardware energy? → If yes, fail (too stiff).
5. Does this sound like IKEA energy? → If yes, fail (too utilitarian).
6. Does this sound like a Sundays Furniture / Quince / Cuyana / Maiden Home brand? → If yes, that's the target.

Any candidate that fails 2 or more of these tests gets dropped before delivery.

## Premium Practical's voice register

The buyer's own language patterns (use these in copy):

- "wears in not out"
- "not throwaway furniture"
- "lasts 10 years"
- "looks grown-up"
- "doesn't look like everyone else's"
- "feels considered"
- "we sat in it before we bought it"
- "showroom price = website price"
- "no upsell"

These came from Switch Interview synthesis. They convert because they're verbatim customer language. Use them as raw material — don't sanitize.

## Common ICP failures

- Targeting demographics ("women 30–55") instead of behavior + psychographic
- Writing for "everyone who buys furniture" instead of the specific person
- Mixing ICPs in one campaign (a Pinterest-save buyer and a contractor-flipper need totally different messages)
- Skipping the explicit "not us" filter
- Letting the ICP drift over time without updating
